The Year of the Shock: War Prices, Cost Anchors, and the Discipline of Not Paying the Premium

Following the outbreak of the conflict in Iran on 28 February 2026 and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the commodities complex is trading with historically elevated geopolitical and speculative premiums. This report evaluates each commodity against its fundamental anchor — the marginal cost of production — to identify where a real margin of safety exists and where investors are merely paying for fear.

01

Executive Summary

2026 is an extraordinary year for commodities — and precisely for that reason, a dangerous year to buy them. The World Bank projects energy prices to rise by roughly 24% this year, the highest level since Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022, while the broad commodities index is expected to increase by about 16%, driven by the war in the Middle East, surging fertilizer prices, and metals at historical highs.

The trigger is well known: the Iran war and the dual blockade of the Strait of Hormuz by the United States and Iran, which has severely restricted flows of crude oil, refined products, and gas from the Persian Gulf. Brent briefly exceeded $115/bbl at the peak of the conflict; gold reached roughly $5,000/oz in February; silver approached $87/oz; and copper set all-time records on both COMEX and the LME.

Today, the dominant story is de-escalation: a fragile but still active ceasefire, a 60-day memorandum of understanding between Washington and Tehran that is nearly agreed, and Iran’s announcement that it has ended its military operations against Israel on 8 June. Brent has already corrected about 20% from its highs — its worst month since the pandemic in May — while silver fell about 22% in one month and gold declined to roughly $4,300, its lowest level since March. At the same time, the macro backdrop has turned hostile for precious metals: U.S. inflation is expected to reach 4.2% in May, the highest level in almost three years; employment remains strong at +172k; and markets are pricing an approximately 70% probability of a Fed rate hike in December.

Value Verdict — Overall View

A value investor does not buy geopolitical premiums; a value investor buys assets near or below their replacement cost. By that standard, most of the complex looks expensive: gold trades at roughly 2.6× its production cost, silver at around 3.4×, and oil remains 40–50% above marginal cost despite a projected supply surplus in Q4 2026. The notable exception is uranium, which is trading almost exactly within its incentive-price band while benefiting from structural demand from nuclear power and AI data centers. Preference hierarchy: uranium > copper on corrections > energy on deep pullbacks > gold > silver.

02

Methodology: What Does “Value” Mean in Commodities?

A commodity does not generate cash flows: there is no DCF to be performed on a bar of metal or a barrel of oil. Graham would classify it as a speculative asset — unless the price is bought at a discount to a verifiable economic anchor. This report uses four anchors, in order of importance:

(1) Marginal cost of production. The 90th percentile of the industry cost curve (AISC for metals; shale breakevens and OPEC fiscal costs for crude; incentive prices for new copper and uranium projects). Over the long run, price gravitates toward the cost of the marginal producer: paying far above that level requires a thesis of permanent scarcity; buying near or below it offers a margin of safety. (2) Physical balance — inventories, supply/demand, spare capacity. (3) Mean reversion in real prices adjusted for inflation. (4) Positioning — when price is supported by speculative flows rather than the physical balance, correction risk dominates.

The cost bands used in this report are the author’s estimates based on industry ranges, including producer-reported AISC figures and cost-curve data from sector research providers. They should be treated as orders of magnitude, not audited figures.

Situation. Crude oil is now unwinding the largest geopolitical premium since 2022. The war, which began on 28 February, and the dual blockade of Hormuz reduced Iranian crude loadings to below 0.3 Mb/d in May, versus 1.7 Mb/d in March, and pushed Brent above $115. Two additional factors add downside pressure: OPEC+ continues to raise quotas (+188k b/d in July), and China has aggressively reduced imports, drawing down inventories rather than buying new barrels.

Value lens. The marginal-cost anchor — U.S. shale breakevens around $45–55, offshore projects and OPEC fiscal thresholds up to roughly $65 — places structural “fair value” well below spot. Fitch expects Hormuz to reopen by late July, Brent to average $87 in 2026, and — crucially — a supply surplus in Q4 2026 as Gulf production normalizes and OPEC+ accelerates. Buying oil today is effectively a bet that the war resumes: that is headline speculation, not investment.

Situation. Gold traded near $5,000 in February amid full war panic and now trades around $4,300, back to December 2025 levels. The correction has two catalysts: Iran–Israel de-escalation removes the safe-haven bid, while the Fed turn removes support on the monetary side. With May inflation expected at 4.2%, the highest in almost three years and driven by energy, and employment remaining strong, markets are pricing roughly a 70% probability of a Fed rate hike in December. Rising real rates plus a stronger dollar (DXY near two-month highs) is the worst macro mix for a non-yielding asset.

Value lens. The average AISC of the gold mining industry is around $1,500–1,700/oz: gold trades at approximately 2.6× its cost of production, a premium that historically requires either massive central-bank buying or an active crisis. The structural argument — de-dollarization and official-sector demand — is real, but it is already more than reflected in the price. For a value investor, gold at these levels protects less than it costs. If hedging exposure is required, low-cost producers and royalty companies offer better economics than the metal: they capture record margins of roughly $2,600/oz above AISC and trade at more reasonable multiples.

Situation. Copper is the only story in the complex where record prices rest on structural demand rather than fear: AI infrastructure and data centers, electrification, and the energy transition are colliding with a mining supply chain that takes 10–15 years to respond. After closing 2025 at $12,423/t on the LME and $5.68/lb on COMEX with +41% gains, copper set records in January on the LME at $14,527/t and in May on COMEX at $6.716/lb, while consolidating near the upper end of the range despite weaker Chinese imports.

Value lens. The incentive price for new greenfield projects is around $4.00–4.50/lb, meaning spot trades at approximately 1.4× that anchor. This is the most defensible multiple in the complex: the structural deficit is plausible, and the true marginal producer — new projects, declining ore grades, permitting delays — becomes more expensive every year. But “structural quality” does not mean “buying at historical highs.” Value discipline requires waiting for corrections toward the incentive band. Accumulation zone: $4.80–5.30/lb. A better vehicle may be low-cost producers with volume growth, which monetize the deficit without paying the record spot price.

Situation. Uranium is consolidating around $86/lb after the speculative rally of early 2026 cooled, with utility buyers relying more on long-term contracts than on the spot market. The structural backdrop is the strongest in decades: Meta and Microsoft have signed agreements to secure nuclear capacity for AI data centers, Italy is advancing a legal framework to reactivate nuclear energy, and volatility in electricity markets, worsened by the war-driven energy shock, is pushing governments toward nuclear baseload generation.

Value lens. Uranium is the only commodity in this coverage trading inside its incentive-price band of roughly $75–90/lb — the level required for new mining projects to reach final investment decision. In other words, the market is not paying a speculative premium: it is paying approximately what it costs to bring on the future supply needed by structural demand. Price ≈ replacement cost + growing and inelastic demand (uranium is only about 5% of the cost of operating a reactor, so demand is not destroyed by higher fuel prices) + multi-decade contractual buyers. This is the closest thing to a Graham-style setup available in the commodities complex in June 2026. Vehicles: physical trust such as Sprott or established low-cost producers; avoid non-producing explorers.

05

Macro Backdrop: The Fed Is the Enemy Again

The energy supply shock is doing what supply shocks do: feeding through into headline inflation. Consensus expects the May U.S. CPI release to rise to 4.2%, the highest level in almost three years, driven by energy. With the labor market adding 172,000 jobs in May, well above expectations, markets have moved from pricing cuts to pricing an approximately 70% probability of a rate hike in December, up from about 50% before the employment data. The dollar, measured by DXY, reacted by rising to nearly two-month highs.

For the commodities complex, this creates the key divergence that structures the entire report: monetary assets such as gold and silver face rising real rates and a stronger dollar — a direct headwind — while physical-balance assets such as copper and uranium depend on industrial supply/demand and are relatively less exposed to the Fed cycle. Oil sits in the middle: its price today is almost entirely a function of Hormuz geopolitics, while the macro picture — weak China and rising OPEC+ supply — points lower once the premium dissipates.

World Bank context (Commodity Markets Outlook, 2 June 2026 update): in May, the energy index fell −5.4% (Brent −10.7%, U.S. natural gas +6.1%); metals rose +3.7%; precious metals fell −1.7%; food rose +1.9%; and fertilizers declined −4.3% after several months of increases. Annual 2026 projection: energy +24%, broad commodities index +16%.

Risk Matrix

Bearish for the complex

Hormuz reopening around late July, according to Fitch

Normalizes roughly 1.4 Mb/d of Iranian loadings and Gulf flows. Fitch sees a supply surplus in Q4 2026 as OPEC+ accelerates. This deflates crude, gas, and by contagion the inflation bid in gold.

Bullish for the complex

Ceasefire breakdown

The agreement is fragile: attacks were exchanged over the weekend of 6–7 June. A full resumption would revive the war premium in crude, plausibly adding $15–25/bbl, and renew the safe-haven bid in precious metals.

Bearish for precious metals

Fed hiking cycle

Expected CPI at 4.2% plus a strong labor market implies a 70% probability of a December hike. Rising real rates and a stronger DXY hurt gold and silver, which already trade at 2.6×–3.4× their cost base.

Bearish for industrial metals

Weak Chinese demand

China has aggressively reduced crude imports by drawing down inventories, and its industrial momentum remains the main uncertainty for copper at historical highs.

Bearish — positioning

Speculative unwind

Silver fell 22% in one month without a major fundamental change, illustrating how much hot money supports precious metals. Further 15–25% corrections are plausible without breaking the underlying “thesis.”

Bullish for uranium

Nuclear-AI acceleration

Each hyperscaler agreement, including Meta and Microsoft, and each new regulatory framework, such as Italy’s, tightens the structural deficit. Fuel demand is price-inelastic.

Suggested Positioning — Framework, Not Recommendation

Value hierarchy across the commodities complex as of June 2026, from most to least attractive on a price/anchor basis:

1

Uranium — Relative overweight The only asset trading at roughly 1.0× its incentive anchor, with growing and inelastic structural demand. Vehicles: physical trust or established low-cost producers. Position size should remain moderate: the spot market is illiquid and volatile.

2

Copper — Accumulate on corrections ($4.80–5.30/lb) The best structural thesis: AI and electrification versus inelastic supply. However, record prices are not an entry point. Prefer low-cost producers with volume growth over futures exposure.

3

Energy — Patience until $65–75 Brent Let the war premium continue to unwind and allow the market to digest the projected Q4 2026 supply surplus. In that range, integrated majors with capital discipline become interesting again, with double-digit FCF yields.

4

Gold — Underweight the metal; if hedging is required, use royalties/producers At 2.6× AISC with the Fed hiking, the metal offers little margin of safety. Producers capture record margins of roughly $2,600/oz and offer better economics.

5

Silver — Avoid / trading only 3.4× its cost anchor, down 22% in one month, and a small market dominated by flows. Volatility is not a margin of safety.

Note for private investors in Switzerland: exposure through producer equities preserves the private investor capital-gains treatment; intensive use of leveraged futures or derivatives may put that tax classification at risk. This is an additional reason — alongside the negative carry of rolling futures — to prefer low-cost equities as the implementation vehicle.
